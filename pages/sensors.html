The math, with real numbers

Ultrasonic: echo time → distance

Sound travels at c = 343 m/s. The pulse goes out and back, so divide the round-trip time by 2. Suppose the HC-SR04 reports an echo of 1166 µs:

distance = c · t / 2
         = 343 m/s · 1166×10⁻⁶ s / 2
         = 0.200 m = 20 cm

Rearranged, that's 58.3 µs per cm (the constant 2 / 343 / 100 in seconds), which is why the snippet above multiplies the raw seconds by 17150 to get centimetres.

Encoder: ticks → angle → distance

A 20-slot disc gives 360° / 20 = 18° per tick. After 75 ticks on a wheel of radius r = 3 cm:

angle    = 75 · 18°       = 1350°  = 3.75 revolutions
circumf. = 2·π·3          = 18.85 cm
distance = (75 / 20) · 18.85 = 70.69 cm

Combining two noisy sensors: the complementary filter

A gyro is smooth but drifts; an accelerometer is noisy but doesn't drift. A complementary filter blends them with one constant α (close to 1) — cheaper than a full Kalman filter and good enough for a tilt estimate:

θ = α·(θ_prev + gyro_rate·dt) + (1−α)·θ_accel

With α = 0.98, θ_prev = 10°, gyro_rate = 4°/s, dt = 0.05 s, and a noisy θ_accel = 12°:

gyro path = 10 + 4·0.05 = 10.20°
θ = 0.98·10.20 + 0.02·12 = 10.24°

The result leans 98% on the trusted gyro integration but lets the accelerometer slowly pull out long-term drift.

Sensor fusion preview — Real robots combine multiple sensors because each one is noisy or limited. Your Pi car will use the ultrasonic to detect obstacles, but it could add IR to follow a line and encoders to know how far it has traveled. The complementary filter above is the simplest fusion; a Kalman filter generalises the same idea to combine all three into one clean estimate of the robot's state.
