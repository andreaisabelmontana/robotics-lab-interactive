⚡ DC Motor + PWM

A DC motor's speed is roughly proportional to voltage. Microcontrollers can't easily vary voltage — they vary the duty cycle of a fast on/off signal (PWM). The motor's inertia smooths the pulses into average power.

Three motor types — which to pick

DC motor

Spins continuously; speed ∝ voltage, set via PWM duty. Cheap and fast but has no position sense on its own — pair it with an encoder to know where it is. This is what drives your Pi car's wheels.

Servo

A DC motor + gearbox + potentiometer + controller in one box. You command an absolute angle (0–180°) with a PWM pulse width; it holds that angle under load. Great for sweeping the ultrasonic sensor.

Stepper

Moves in fixed steps by energising coils in sequence — open-loop yet precise. A common 200-step motor turns 360 / 200 = 1.8° per step (0.9° in half-step mode). Used in 3D printers and CNC where exact, repeatable position matters.

PWM duty cycle → effective voltage

A DC motor can't easily take a variable voltage, so the driver switches the full supply on and off quickly. The motor's inertia averages the pulses, so the effective voltage is just duty × Vsupply. On a 6 V pack:

duty = 25%  →  Veff = 0.25 · 6 = 1.50 V   (crawl)
duty = 50%  →  Veff = 0.50 · 6 = 3.00 V   (half speed)
duty = 75%  →  Veff = 0.75 · 6 = 4.50 V   (most of full speed)

Drag the Duty cycle slider on the DC motor above and the "Effective voltage" readout tracks exactly this calculation.

How they combine in the Pi car: two DC gear motors (one per side, differential drive) controlled by an L298N H-bridge. The Pi sends two GPIO signals per motor (forward/reverse) plus one PWM (speed). A single servo, optionally, can sweep an ultrasonic sensor side-to-side to scan for obstacles.
